# Core Audience Principle

The website should work for multiple audiences, but it should not flatten itself to please all of them.

The primary reader is a senior decision-maker evaluating whether Nikhil can operate at Head / Director / VP-level design and product leadership.

The site should be clear enough for a recruiter, deep enough for a VP, useful enough for a design leader, and credible enough for founders, product leaders, engineering leaders, and AI-native teams.

---

# Primary Audience

## 1. VP Design / Chief Design Officer / Design Executive

This is the most important audience.

They are evaluating:

> Can this person build and lead a serious product design organization?

They care about:

- Organizational design
- Hiring
- Team maturity
- Design quality
- Cross-functional influence
- Product judgment
- Operating rituals
- Research maturity
- Executive communication
- Ability to scale teams and systems
- Ability to operate under ambiguity
- Ability to create business impact

They are not impressed by:

- Pretty screens without decision context
- Generic UX process
- Tool fluency
- Trendy AI language
- Overdesigned portfolio visuals
- Case studies that hide tradeoffs
- Claims without evidence

They need to believe:

> Nikhil can build the operating system of a product design organization.

Proof they need:

- Design function built or scaled
- Team rituals introduced
- Hiring and leadership systems
- Research practice
- Design systems
- Product reviews
- Executive alignment
- Transformation stories
- Metrics
- Lessons learned
- What he would do differently

Best content for them:

- Building Design Organization transformation story
- AI-native Product Development transformation story
- Operating Manual
- Hiring
- Design Reviews
- Product Reviews
- Decision Making
- Research
- Design Systems
- Leadership essays

Desired emotional response:

> This person thinks like a design executive, not just a senior IC or manager.

- Business outcomes

# Audience Risks

## Risk 1 — Too abstract for recruiters

Mitigation:

- Clear title
- Clear scope
- Metrics
- Resume link
- Contact CTA

## Risk 2 — Too portfolio-like for executives

Mitigation:

- Transformation stories
- Operating Manual
- Decisions
- Tradeoffs
- Systems
- Outcomes

## Risk 3 — Too self-promotional for serious readers

Mitigation:

- Teach before impressing
- Evidence over adjectives
- Lessons
- What I’d Do Differently

## Risk 4 — Too dense for quick scanning

Mitigation:

- Layered experience
- Strong hierarchy
- Executive snapshot
- Scannable metrics
- Clear navigation

## Risk 5 — Too AI-hype for product leaders

Mitigation:

- AI as operating model
- AI workflow examples
- Clear before/after states
- Avoid buzzwords without proof

## Risk 6 — Too founder-side-project-heavy

Mitigation:

- Frame LANE and Svenklas as builder/operator proof
- Do not let them overpower enterprise leadership
- Connect them back to operating philosophy

- design operating system

# Audience Exclusions

This site is not primarily for:

- Junior designers looking for basic UX process
- Clients looking for freelance UI design
- Recruiters hiring mid-level IC designers
- People looking for visual inspiration only
- Trend followers
- Generic content consumers
- People looking for tool tutorials
- People looking for shallow AI prompts
- People looking for portfolio templates only

They may still find value, but they are not the design target.

---

# Final Audience Rule

Design for the senior reader.

Make it clear enough for the recruiter.

Make it deep enough for the VP.

Make it useful enough for the designer.

Make it credible enough for the founder.

That is the audience strategy.
